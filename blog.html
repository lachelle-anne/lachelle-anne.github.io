D E V E L O P I N G

Whether a beginner, student, graduate, junior or senior, gaining access to educational content and identifying resources appropriate to your level whilst also meeting your requirements can often be tricky, especially starting out.

When browsing through any search engine, the first links available when searching ‘learn to code’ are listings by larger companies offering fast-tracked courses, boot-camps, classes and subscriptions. Before it is said, yes, trying a similar search for information on other industries like this will often obtain the same result. The internet is vast and searches can often be incoherent or imprecise due the phrasing or vagueness of your search. This can often be confronting and deterring as a beginner, and like most things takes time and patience to understand and define.

So, where do you start?

The first thing worth noting is that there are many different learning styles. Quite often we hear about them in passing, but not all content caters for multiple learning styles. Being able to first identify your learning style - or even styles - is key in optimising your learning. This will enable you to build techniques to better engage and understand the information given, not only aiding you for your studies, but equipping you with skills that can be utilised within your career.

The next part can be a bit more challenging – sourcing information which is relevant to the level you are at. Instances of courses and video tutorials are easily accessible but can often be daunting, and if intended at a higher level audience, the experience can be disengaging and demotivate your enthusiasm to explore more into the subject.

Starting out with resources that provide a basic hands-on approach such as online code editors and interactive tutorial sections, which allow you to work with and edit code, will assist you in gaining a better understanding of what code is and how changes within it affects the result.

When I first began looking into programming, one of the first recommendations online mentioned to have a project in mind, and choose a programming language. This can definitely be great incentive to begin learning and work on your own passion project, but not all will start with this direction. As you become more attuned to programming, you will find that some ideas can be complex to start with, or may have another programming language more suitable for the project you have in mind – but don’t let this deter you from trying!

When looking to start researching and learning a language, a guide which ranks them in order of difficulty or specifies easier languages to start with as a beginner is a great way to commence learning. There are a few websites worth mentioning which provide a great introduction to a range of programming languages and are easy to navigate and follow – I will list a few at the end of this blog entry which I find quite insightful and use regularly for guidance.

Starting with a simple project that has been covered well and using your resources as a guide rather than following a video or tutorial step-by-step will ultimately benefit your learning, encouraging your involvement and problem solving skills, rather than relying on mimicking or memorising the session. Not only does this help you understand the code, but you feel more achieved in knowing that you figured it out – puzzles are your friends!

A solution can be implemented in many different ways and refined as you learn more. Don’t let a challenge stop you from achieving knowledge.

You don't have to be great to start, but you have to start to be great.

– Zig Ziglar
